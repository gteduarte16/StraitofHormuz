STRAIT OF HORMUZ: TIMELINE AND STATUS
Overview

The Strait of Hormuz has been a primary flashpoint in the 2026 conflict between the United States, Israel, and Iran, following the launch of Operation Epic Fury on February 28, 2026.

The waterway has fluctuated between complete closure and "selective passage" for specific nations.

Current Status

As of Apr 24, 2026: The Strait remained effectively closed to Western traffic, with mine-clearing expected to take months after hostilities.

Strait of Hormuz – 2026 Conflict Highlights
  1. Feb 15–20, 2026: Iran reportedly tripled oil exports and reduced storage in anticipation of conflict.
  2. Feb 28: The U.S. and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury; Iran immediately announced the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.
  3. Early March: Around 150 vessels were reported waiting outside the waterway as traffic was disrupted.
  4. Mar 2: Iran officially declared the Strait closed to all traffic.
  5. Mar 4–8: Global shipping through the Strait sharply declined; Brent crude rose above $100/barrel.
  6. Mar 5: Iran shifted to a selective blockade, reportedly allowing some non-Western vessels while restricting Western-linked ships.
  7. Mar 10: U.S. intelligence reported Iran had begun laying sea mines in the Strait.
  8. Mar 19: The U.S. began military operations to re-open the waterway.
  9. Mar 26: Iran reportedly allowed passage only for ships linked to China, Russia, India, Iraq, and Pakistan.
  10. Apr 8: A two-week provisional ceasefire temporarily raised hopes of reopening.
  11. Apr 18: After failed negotiations and continuing tensions, Iran re-closed the Strait.
  12. Apr 22: Two commercial ships, Epaminondas and MSC Francesca, were reportedly seized off Oman.

Crude Oil and Petroleum Liquids Transported Through World Chokepoints

Major maritime chokepoints account for approximately 91.7% of global maritime oil trade, highlighting their strategic importance to global energy supply chains.

Strait of Hormuz

In 1H 2025, the Strait of Hormuz carried around 20.9 million b/d of crude oil, condensate, and petroleum products, representing over one-fifth of global oil consumption. About 89% of crude oil and condensate flows were bound for Asia, mainly China, India, Japan, and South Korea.

Strait of Malacca

The Strait of Malacca, between Malaysia and Indonesia, was the world’s busiest oil chokepoint, with about 23.2 million b/d passing through in 1H 2025, equivalent to around 29.1% of global maritime oil trade.

Strategic Oil Inventories

As of April 20, 2026, EIA data indicate that China, the United States, and Japan held the largest strategic oil inventories in 2025.
